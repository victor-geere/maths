# Projective Space & Projective Varieties

**Projective space** $\mathbb{P}^n$ extends affine $n$-space by adding **points at infinity** — one for each direction. Two parallel lines in $\mathbb{A}^2$ meet at a point at infinity in $\mathbb{P}^2$; two conics always meet in exactly 4 points (counted with multiplicity) in $\mathbb{P}^2$ by Bézout's theorem. This compactification gives algebraic varieties much better properties: projective varieties are compact (in the classical topology over $\mathbb{C}$), and intersection theory behaves uniformly without exceptions for parallel lines. Most varieties studied in algebraic geometry are projective or open subsets thereof, and the transition between affine and projective patches is the fundamental technique for studying global geometry via local algebraic methods.

## Projective $n$-Space

$$\mathbb{P}^n_k = (k^{n+1} \setminus \{0\}) / \sim$$

where $(a_0,\ldots,a_n) \sim (\lambda a_0,\ldots,\lambda a_n)$ for all $\lambda \in k^*$.

Points are written as **homogeneous coordinates** $[a_0 : a_1 : \cdots : a_n]$.

## Affine Charts

$\mathbb{P}^n$ is covered by $n+1$ affine charts: the $i$-th chart is $U_i = \{a_i \neq 0\} \cong \mathbb{A}^n$ via $[a_0:\cdots:a_n] \mapsto (a_0/a_i, \ldots, \hat{a}_i/a_i, \ldots, a_n/a_i)$.

## Homogeneous Polynomials

A polynomial $F \in k[X_0,\ldots,X_n]$ is **homogeneous** of degree $d$ if $F(\lambda X_0,\ldots,\lambda X_n) = \lambda^d F(X_0,\ldots,X_n)$. While $F(P)$ is not well-defined on points of $\mathbb{P}^n$, the zero locus $\{F = 0\}$ is well-defined.

## Projective Varieties

A **projective variety** $V \subseteq \mathbb{P}^n$ is the common zero set of a collection of homogeneous polynomials:

$$V = V(F_1, \ldots, F_r) = \{[a_0:\cdots:a_n] \in \mathbb{P}^n : F_i(a_0,\ldots,a_n)=0 \text{ for all }i\}$$

## Homogeneous Ideal

The **homogeneous ideal** of $V$ is the ideal $I(V) \subseteq k[X_0,\ldots,X_n]$ generated by all homogeneous polynomials vanishing on $V$.

## Key Properties

- $\mathbb{P}^n$ over $\mathbb{C}$ is compact (closed and bounded as a manifold)
- Every projective variety over $\mathbb{C}$ is compact
- Bézout's theorem holds cleanly in projective space

## Glossary

| Term / Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| $\mathbb{P}^n_k$ | projective $n$-space over $k$ |
| $[a_0:\cdots:a_n]$ | homogeneous coordinates of a point in $\mathbb{P}^n$ |
| $\sim$ | equivalence: $(a_0,\ldots,a_n) \sim (\lambda a_0,\ldots,\lambda a_n)$ for $\lambda \neq 0$ |
| $k^*$ | the multiplicative group of non-zero elements of $k$ |
| Affine chart $U_i$ | the open subset $\{a_i \neq 0\} \cong \mathbb{A}^n$ |
| $\hat{a}_i$ | the coordinate $a_i$ is omitted |
| Homogeneous polynomial | $F(\lambda \mathbf{x}) = \lambda^d F(\mathbf{x})$; well-defines a zero locus on $\mathbb{P}^n$ |
| Degree $d$ | the degree of the homogeneous polynomial |
| Projective variety | zero locus of homogeneous polynomials in $\mathbb{P}^n$ |
| Compact | in the classical (Euclidean) topology: closed and bounded |
| Points at infinity | the complement $\mathbb{P}^n \setminus \mathbb{A}^n$; a hyperplane |
