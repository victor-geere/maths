# Special Angles (30¬į, 45¬į, 60¬į)

The angles $30¬į$, $45¬į$, and $60¬į$ (equivalently $\pi/6$, $\pi/4$, $\pi/3$ radians) are called **special angles** because their trigonometric values can be expressed exactly in terms of simple surds rather than requiring a calculator. They arise naturally from two elementary triangles ‚ÄĒ the half-equilateral triangle and the isosceles right triangle ‚ÄĒ and they appear constantly in geometry, physics, and calculus. Memorising or rapidly reconstructing these values is a core skill in any quantitative discipline.

## Derivation from Two Triangles

### The 45¬į‚Äď45¬į‚Äď90¬į Triangle
Take an isosceles right triangle with legs of length $1$. The hypotenuse is $\sqrt{2}$ (by Pythagoras).

$$\sin 45¬į = \cos 45¬į = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}, \qquad \tan 45¬į = 1$$

### The 30¬į‚Äď60¬į‚Äď90¬į Triangle
Bisect an equilateral triangle of side $2$. This creates a right triangle with hypotenuse $2$, short leg $1$, and long leg $\sqrt{3}$.

$$\sin 30¬į = \frac{1}{2},\quad \cos 30¬į = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2},\quad \tan 30¬į = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}$$

$$\sin 60¬į = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2},\quad \cos 60¬į = \frac{1}{2},\quad \tan 60¬į = \sqrt{3}$$

## Memory Aid

The values of $\sin$ at $0¬į, 30¬į, 45¬į, 60¬į, 90¬į$ follow the pattern:

$$\frac{\sqrt{0}}{2},\quad \frac{\sqrt{1}}{2},\quad \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2},\quad \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2},\quad \frac{\sqrt{4}}{2}$$

For $\cos$, the same sequence runs in reverse.
