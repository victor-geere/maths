# Pythagorean Theorem

The **Pythagorean theorem** is one of the most celebrated results in all of mathematics: in any right triangle, the area of the square built on the hypotenuse equals the combined area of the squares on the two shorter sides. Known to the Babylonians over 3,000 years ago and proved by Euclid, it is the direct origin of the distance formula in any number of dimensions, the definition of the trigonometric functions, and the inner product on Euclidean space. It has more known proofs than any other theorem — over 370 by some counts.

## Statement

For a right triangle with legs $a$ and $b$ and hypotenuse $c$ (the side opposite the right angle):

$$a^2 + b^2 = c^2$$

## Proof by Rearrangement

Draw a square of side $(a+b)$. Inside, place four copies of the right triangle, leaving a square of side $c$ in the middle.

Area equation:

$$(a+b)^2 = 4 \cdot \frac{ab}{2} + c^2 \implies a^2 + 2ab + b^2 = 2ab + c^2 \implies a^2 + b^2 = c^2 \quad \square$$

## Converse

If $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$ for the three sides of a triangle, then the angle opposite $c$ is exactly $90°$. This is used to check whether a triangle is right-angled.

## Pythagorean Triples

Integer solutions to $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$:

| $a$ | $b$ | $c$ |
|---|---|---|
| 3 | 4 | 5 |
| 5 | 12 | 13 |
| 8 | 15 | 17 |
| 7 | 24 | 25 |

All triples are generated by: $a = m^2 - n^2$, $b = 2mn$, $c = m^2 + n^2$ for integers $m > n > 0$.

## Generalisations

**In $n$ dimensions:** $d = \sqrt{x_1^2 + x_2^2 + \cdots + x_n^2}$ (Euclidean distance from the origin).

**Law of Cosines** (general triangle, angle $C$ opposite side $c$):

$$c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab\cos C$$

The Pythagorean theorem is the special case $C = 90°$.

## Glossary

| Term / Symbol | Meaning |
|---|---|
| $a, b$ | legs — the two shorter sides of a right triangle |
| $c$ | hypotenuse — the side opposite the right angle |
| $90°$ / $\pi/2$ | a right angle |
| Pythagorean triple | a set of three positive integers satisfying $a^2+b^2=c^2$ |
| Converse | the reverse implication: $a^2+b^2=c^2 \Rightarrow$ right angle at $C$ |
| Law of Cosines | $c^2 = a^2+b^2-2ab\cos C$ — generalises Pythagoras to any triangle |
| Euclidean distance | straight-line distance; in 2D equals $\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2}$ |
| Rearrangement proof | proof by dissecting and rearranging areas |
| $m, n$ | integers used to generate Pythagorean triples |
