ABSTRACT
We examine two intensionally distinct yet extensionally equivalent recursive definitions of the triangular number function: the direct accumulation f(n) = f(n−1) + n with f(0) = 0, and the auxiliary-square formulation r(n) = g(n) − r(n−1) with g(n) = g(n−1) + 2n − 1. Both compute n(n+1)/2. We provide a rigorous formalization of this distinction between symbolic (intensional) and input–output (extensional) equality. We then reduce the problem of detecting whether a third, differently defined recursive function h is inequivalent to a family of Boolean satisfiability (SAT) instances via bounded unrolling and bit-blasting. Finally, we explore k-induction as a powerful automated technique to prove full equivalence when it holds, encoding both base cases and inductive steps as SMT queries. Concrete examples, including a shifted triangular-number function that diverges at n=1, illustrate the methodology. The work unifies classical mathematical proof, modern satisfiability solving, and automated induction into a coherent framework for reasoning about recursive functions.

01 Introduction

Functions in mathematics and computer science can be presented in multiple ways. Two definitions may appear completely different—using distinct recursive structures, auxiliary variables, or computational strategies—yet produce identical results for every input. This phenomenon raises fundamental questions about the nature of equality: when are two functions “the same”?

We begin with a concrete and illuminating pair of recursive definitions over the natural numbers (including zero). The first is the classic accumulation that directly builds triangular numbers. The second uses an auxiliary function that computes perfect squares and then applies a subtraction-based recurrence. Although their defining equations differ profoundly, both compute exactly the same values. A third function h, defined by yet another recurrence, raises the practical question: how can we rigorously determine whether it is equivalent to the first two, and if not, what function it actually computes?

Motivating Observation

The functions are intensionally distinct (different symbolic presentations and algorithms) yet extensionally equal (identical input–output behavior). This distinction is central to logic, type theory, program verification, and the philosophy of mathematics.

This paper makes three contributions that together provide a complete methodological toolkit:

  1. A precise mathematical formalization of the original statement distinguishing intensional and extensional equality.
  2. A reduction of the problem of detecting inequivalence with a third function h to Boolean Satisfiability (SAT) via bounded model checking.
  3. An exploration of k-induction as an automated technique for proving full equivalence when it holds, including its encoding as SMT queries and its relationship to classical induction and bounded SAT search.

02 Background: The Functions and Their Equivalence

FUNCTION f
Direct triangular accumulation
\[ f(0) = 0, \quad f(n) = f(n-1) + n \quad (n \geq 1) \]

This is the standard primitive-recursive definition of the sum of the first n natural numbers.

FUNCTIONS g AND r
Auxiliary-square formulation
\[ \begin{align*} g(0) &= 0, & g(n) &= g(n-1) + 2n - 1 \quad (n \geq 1) \\ r(0) &= 0, & r(n) &= g(n) - r(n-1) \quad (n \geq 1) \end{align*} \]

Here g computes perfect squares while r uses subtraction in a two-phase recurrence.

Closed-Form Equivalence

Both functions compute the nth triangular number:

\[ f(n) = r(n) = \frac{n(n+1)}{2} \]

This can be shown by induction on n (detailed in Section 4). The equality demonstrates that two very different recursive presentations can define identical functions extensionally.

03 Formalization of the Original Statement

Let \(\mathbb{N}_0 = \{0, 1, 2, \dots\}\). We formalize the two functions and the distinction between their presentations.

Definition 3.1 (Intensional vs. Extensional Equality)

Two total functions \(f, r : \mathbb{N}_0 \to \mathbb{N}_0\) are intensionally distinct if their defining recursive equations differ syntactically and cannot be transformed into each other by mere unfolding or equational rewriting. They are extensionally equal (written \(f = r\)) if and only if they determine the same set of ordered pairs:

\[ \forall n \in \mathbb{N}_0.\quad f(n) = r(n) \]

The original statement asserts precisely that f and r are intensionally distinct (different recursive schemes, one direct and one via an auxiliary square function) yet extensionally equal. Consequently they are the same function when viewed as mathematical objects (graphs).

For a third function h given by any other recursive specification, the central questions become:

  • How can we determine whether \(\forall n.\ h(n) = f(n)\)?
  • If not, what function does h actually compute?

04 Reduction to Boolean Satisfiability

The full statement \(\forall n.\ h(n) = f(n)\) is a \(\Pi_1\) sentence and cannot be decided by a single finite SAT instance. However, the problem of refuting equivalence (finding a concrete counterexample) reduces elegantly to SAT.

Bounded Counterexample Search as SAT

For chosen bounds \(N\) (maximum \(n\)) and bit-width \(b\) (sufficient to represent all values up to \(N\)), we construct a CNF formula \(\phi(N, b)\):

VARIABLES
  • • Bit-vector variables for \(f(m)\) and \(h(m)\) (\(m = 0 \dots N\))
  • • Auxiliary gate variables for arithmetic circuits
CONSTRAINTS
  • • Base cases encoded as unit clauses
  • • Recursive steps encoded as adder/subtractor circuits (CNF)
  • • Difference assertion: \(\bigvee_m (f(m) \neq h(m))\)

Interpretation

  • SAT → Concrete counterexample \(n \leq N\) exists (with witness values). h is not equivalent.
  • UNSAT → No difference up to \(N\). Increase \(N\) iteratively (bounded model checking style).

This encoding is the foundation of Bounded Model Checking applied to functional equivalence. It provides a sound semi-decision procedure for inequivalence.

Concrete Illustration (Shifted Triangular Function)

Consider the intensionally distinct function:

\[ h(0) = 0, \quad h(n) = h(n-1) + (n + 1) \quad (n \geq 1) \]

Using the SAT encoding with \(N=5\), \(b=16\) (via Z3 bit-vector theory, which compiles to SAT), the instance is satisfiable. The model yields:

05 k-Induction for Equivalence Proofs

k-induction generalizes classical mathematical induction and is particularly effective when encoded as SMT queries. It enables automated proofs of properties over infinite domains by checking a finite base and a single inductive step that assumes k previous states.

Definition 5.1 (k-Induction)

To prove \(\forall n.\ P(n)\):

  • Base: Prove \(P(0) \land P(1) \land \dots \land P(k-1)\).
  • Step: Prove \(\forall n \geq k-1.\ (P(n-k+1) \land \dots \land P(n)) \implies P(n+1)\).

If both hold, then \(P(n)\) is true for all \(n\) by generalized induction.

Application to Function Equivalence

Let \(P(n) \equiv h(n) = f(n)\). Because the definitions involve auxiliary state (e.g., \(g(n)\)), we usually strengthen to a joint invariant:

\[ Q(n) \equiv \bigl(g(n) = n^2\bigr) \land \bigl(r(n) = \tfrac{n(n+1)}{2}\bigr) \land \bigl(h(n) = \tfrac{n(n+1)}{2}\bigr) \]

The base and inductive step are encoded exactly as bounded unrollings (identical technology to the SAT refutation encoding), but the queries now ask whether the negation of the desired property is unsatisfiable under the assumptions.

Key Insight

k-induction re-uses the same circuit unrolling and bit-blasting infrastructure developed for SAT-based counterexample search, but turns the checks into proofs rather than bug hunts. For many simple recursive arithmetic functions, k=1 (classical induction) already suffices and can be fully automated.

06 Worked Examples and Comparison of Methods

Classical Induction
Manual / Theorem Prover

Directly prove base and step by hand or in Isabelle/Coq. Excellent when closed forms or simple invariants are obvious (as with \(f = r\)).

Bounded SAT Search
Automated Refutation

Quickly finds small counterexamples when they exist. Sound for disproving equivalence; incomplete for proving it.

k-Induction
Automated Proof

Best of both worlds for many cases. Automates the inductive step via SMT while guaranteeing full equivalence when successful.

Workflow recommendation: Use bounded SAT first to hunt for counterexamples. If none appear up to a large bound, attempt k-induction (starting with k=1 or 2). If successful, equivalence is proved. If it fails, derive a closed form for h and prove the relation classically.

07 Discussion, Nuances & Implications

Edge Cases and Limitations

  • Bit-width overflow: Must choose \(b\) conservatively; modular arithmetic can mask true behavior.
  • Completeness of k-induction: Not always complete. Some properties require property strengthening or increasing k.
  • State complexity: Mutual recursion or many auxiliaries increases the size of the unrolled formula.
  • Non-arithmetic definitions: Still encodable if control flow can be expressed with circuits (multiplexers, etc.).

Broader Implications

This methodology directly supports translation validation, compiler correctness, and equivalence checking of optimized implementations. It also illustrates the deep connection between classical mathematics (induction, closed forms) and modern automated reasoning (SAT, SMT, bounded model checking). The intensional/extensional distinction remains philosophically important: SAT and k-induction operate on the operational (intensional) definitions yet conclude extensional equality.

Conclusion

We have provided a unified treatment of equivalence for recursive functions. The original statement was formalized by distinguishing intensional presentations from extensional graphs. The problem of detecting inequivalence with a third function was reduced to a family of Boolean satisfiability instances. Finally, k-induction was explored as a practical, automatable technique for proving full equivalence. Together these methods—classical mathematics, SAT-based refutation, and k-induction—form a robust and complementary toolkit. When equivalence holds, k-induction (or classical induction) yields a proof; when it fails, bounded SAT search quickly supplies a concrete counterexample and guides identification of the actual function computed by h.
