The Unnormalised Prime Kernel
A Positive-Definite Reproducing Kernel Carried by the Primes

Research note — 3 July 2026

Abstract

We construct the reproducing kernel Hilbert space naturally associated with the primes: the Gram kernel of the vectors \(p^{-1/2+i\lambda}\) in \(\ell^2(\text{primes})\), a positive-definite kernel on the upper half-plane equal to a shifted prime zeta function. This kernel is exact — unlike the finite, \(1/\pi(M_n)\)-normalised prime sums used in several recent Hilbert–Pólya proposals, which converge to the zero kernel on any fixed strip (see the companion errata, barry-keating-addendum-errata.html, Lemma L3). We identify its associated Hilbert space of prime Dirichlet series, show that the natural translation flow on this space is unitary with self-adjoint generator equal to multiplication by \(\log p\) — pure point spectrum, one eigenvalue per prime, each simple — and record the boundary behaviour of the kernel as the half-plane parameter degenerates to the real line, where it develops a logarithmic singularity governed by the pole of \(\zeta\) at \(s=1\). All identities are verified numerically to at least \(10^{-7}\), and the log-singularity constant is identified in closed form and checked to 12 digits. We close by placing this kernel in relation to the explicit formula and to the positivity criteria that are unconditionally equivalent to the Riemann Hypothesis (companion note hermite-biehler.html).

1. Introduction

Several recent approaches to the Riemann Hypothesis via Hilbert–Pólya-type constructions build a "prime kernel" as a finite average \(K_n(\lambda,\mu) = \frac1{\pi(M_n)}\sum_{p\le M_n} p^{-1+i(\lambda-\bar\mu)}\) and attempt to pass to a limit \(n\to\infty\) that is meant to reproduce the de Branges kernel of the Riemann \(\Xi\) function. This normalisation is fatal: for \(u = \lambda-\bar\mu\), the sum \(\sum_{p\le M} p^{-1+iu}\) is \(O(\log\log M)\) on \(\mathrm{Im}\,u \ge 0\) and \(\asymp M^{\delta}/\delta\) for \(\mathrm{Im}\,u = -\delta \in (-1,0)\) (Chebyshev bounds), while the normalising factor \(\pi(M_n) \sim M_n/\log M_n\) grows almost linearly in \(M_n\). Dividing by it kills the kernel identically on any fixed strip \(|\mathrm{Im}\,u| < 1-\varepsilon\), and makes it diverge just outside. There is no rescaling of a finite truncation of the prime sum that survives this limit with a nonzero, translation-covariant result.

The resolution is to stop truncating and stop normalising. The infinite prime sum \(\sum_p p^{-1+iu}\) converges absolutely — unconditionally, with no limiting procedure required — as soon as \(\mathrm{Im}\,u > 0\), i.e. as soon as the kernel's two arguments lie in the open upper half-plane. This is not a new function: it is (a shift of) the classical prime zeta function. What is new to record here is the reproducing-kernel-Hilbert-space package built on top of it, its exact translation-flow spectral theory, and its precise boundary behaviour — the three ingredients a future construction on the "prime side" of Hilbert–Pólya needs.

2. Definition and basic positivity

Let \(\mathbb C^+ = \{\lambda \in \mathbb C : \mathrm{Im}\,\lambda > 0\}\). For \(\lambda \in \mathbb C^+\) define the sequence indexed by primes \[ v_\lambda = \big(p^{-1/2+i\lambda}\big)_{p\ \text{prime}} \in \ell^2(\text{primes}). \]

Lemma 2.1 (well-definedness).

\(v_\lambda \in \ell^2(\text{primes})\) for every \(\lambda \in \mathbb C^+\), and \(\lambda \mapsto v_\lambda\) is a holomorphic \(\ell^2\)-valued map on \(\mathbb C^+\).

Proof. \(|p^{-1/2+i\lambda}| = p^{-1/2}\,|p^{i\lambda}| = p^{-1/2-\mathrm{Im}\,\lambda}\), so \(\|v_\lambda\|_{\ell^2}^2 = \sum_p p^{-1-2\,\mathrm{Im}\,\lambda}\), which converges precisely when \(1+2\,\mathrm{Im}\,\lambda > 1\), i.e. \(\mathrm{Im}\,\lambda > 0\). Holomorphy is termwise differentiation of an absolutely and locally uniformly convergent series (dominated by the same bound on compact subsets of \(\mathbb C^+\)). \(\blacksquare\)

Definition 2.2 (the prime kernel).

\[ K(\lambda,\mu) \;:=\; \langle v_\lambda, v_\mu\rangle_{\ell^2} \;=\; \sum_p p^{-1/2+i\lambda}\,\overline{p^{-1/2+i\mu}} \;=\; \sum_p p^{-1+i(\lambda-\bar\mu)}, \qquad \lambda,\mu \in \mathbb C^+. \]

As a Gram kernel of vectors in a Hilbert space, \(K\) is automatically positive semi-definite: for any \(\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_n \in \mathbb C^+\) and \(c_1,\dots,c_n \in \mathbb C\), \(\sum_{i,j} c_i\bar c_j\, K(\lambda_i,\lambda_j) = \big\|\sum_i c_i v_{\lambda_i}\big\|^2 \ge 0\). This is the entire content of positivity here — it requires no analytic input, unlike the finite normalised kernels, whose positivity survives truncation but is destroyed by the limit.

Domain remark. The defining series converges whenever \(\mathrm{Im}\,\lambda + \mathrm{Im}\,\mu > 0\) — a strictly larger region than the product \(\mathbb C^+\times\mathbb C^+\), since one argument may dip slightly below the real axis if the other compensates. We keep to \(\mathbb C^+\times\mathbb C^+\) so that \(K\) is manifestly a Gram kernel; the larger domain is where \(K\) continues as a holomorphic function of two variables, short of positive-definiteness.

3. Identification with the prime zeta function

Proposition 3.1.

\(K(\lambda,\mu) = P\big(1-i(\lambda-\bar\mu)\big)\), where \(P(s) = \sum_p p^{-s}\) is the prime zeta function (absolutely convergent for \(\mathrm{Re}\,s>1\)).

Proof. With \(u = \lambda - \bar\mu\), \(\mathrm{Re}\big(1-iu\big) = 1+\mathrm{Im}\,u = 1+\mathrm{Im}\,\lambda+\mathrm{Im}\,\mu>1\) on \(\mathbb C^+\times\mathbb C^+\), and \(\sum_p p^{-1+iu} = \sum_p p^{-(1-iu)} = P(1-iu)\) term by term. \(\blacksquare\)

\(P\) itself continues meromorphically past the line \(\mathrm{Re}\,s=1\) via the Möbius expansion \(P(s) = \sum_{k\ge1} \frac{\mu(k)}{k}\log\zeta(ks)\) (valid for \(\mathrm{Re}\,s>0\), away from the singular points), with logarithmic branch points at \(s = 1/k\) for each \(k\ge1\) and at every point \(s = \rho/k\) where \(\zeta\) vanishes. The dominant singularity on \(\mathrm{Re}\,s = 1\) is at \(s=1\) itself (the pole of \(\zeta\)), where $$P(s) = -\log(s-1) + c_0 + o(1), \qquad s\to1^+, \qquad c_0 = \sum_{k\ge2}\frac{\mu(k)}{k}\log\zeta(k).$$ This follows termwise: the \(k=1\) term is \(\log\zeta(s) = -\log(s-1)+O(s-1)\) since \((s-1)\zeta(s)\to1\); every \(k\ge2\) term is regular at \(s=1\) (evaluated at \(\zeta(k)\), \(k>1\) real) and contributes its value at \(s=1\) to the constant term.

Consequence for the kernel. As \(\lambda,\mu \to t\in\mathbb R\) with \(\lambda=\mu\) along the diagonal, \(1-i(\lambda-\bar\mu) \to 1\), and \(K\) develops a logarithmic singularity there: \(K(\lambda,\lambda) \sim -\log\big(2\,\mathrm{Im}\,\lambda\big) + c_0\) as \(\mathrm{Im}\,\lambda \downarrow 0\) (using \(1-i(\lambda-\bar\lambda) = 1+2\,\mathrm{Im}\,\lambda\)). This is the exact, boundary-approach analogue of the Szegő-kernel-type logarithmic blow-up expected of a reproducing kernel whose space touches the real line — and it is a genuine singularity of a convergent object, not an artefact of a broken limiting procedure.

4. The Hilbert space of prime Dirichlet series

Definition 4.1.

For \(w \in \ell^2(\text{primes})\) define \(F_w(\lambda) = \sum_p \overline{w_p}\,p^{-1/2+i\lambda} = \langle v_\lambda, w\rangle\), holomorphic on \(\mathbb C^+\). Let \(\mathcal H_P = \{F_w : w \in \ell^2(\text{primes})\}\) with \(\|F_w\|_{\mathcal H_P} := \|w\|_{\ell^2}\).

The map \(w \mapsto F_w\) is by construction an isometric isomorphism \(\ell^2(\text{primes}) \to \mathcal H_P\) (it is literally the inclusion \(w \mapsto \langle v_\bullet, w\rangle\)), so \(\mathcal H_P\) is a Hilbert space of entire — in fact holomorphic on \(\mathbb C^+\) — functions with reproducing kernel exactly \(K\): \(\langle F_w, K(\cdot,\mu)\rangle_{\mathcal H_P} = F_w(\mu)\) for all \(w,\mu\), since \(K(\cdot,\mu) = F_{v_\mu}\) and \(\langle F_w, F_{v_\mu}\rangle_{\mathcal H_P} = \langle w, v_\mu\rangle_{\ell^2} = \overline{\langle v_\mu, w\rangle} = \overline{F_w(\mu)}\) — matching the usual reproducing convention up to the standard conjugate-linearity bookkeeping of the sesquilinear pairing.

5. The translation flow and its generator

For \(t \in \mathbb R\) define the shift \((U_t F)(\lambda) = F(\lambda + t)\) on functions holomorphic on \(\mathbb C^+\). Under the isomorphism of §4, \(U_t\) corresponds on \(\ell^2(\text{primes})\) to the diagonal unitary \((\Lambda_t w)_p = p^{it}\,w_p\), since \(v_{\lambda+t}\big)_p = p^{-1/2+i(\lambda+t)} = p^{it}\,(v_\lambda)_p\).

Proposition 5.1 (spectral theorem for the flow).

\(\{\Lambda_t\}_{t\in\mathbb R}\) is a strongly continuous one-parameter unitary group on \(\ell^2(\text{primes})\). Its self-adjoint generator \(A\) (via \(\Lambda_t = e^{itA}\)) is the multiplication operator \((Aw)_p = (\log p)\,w_p\) on the domain \(\{w : \sum_p (\log p)^2 |w_p|^2 < \infty\}\). The spectrum of \(A\) is \(\overline{\{\log p : p\ \text{prime}\}}\), consisting of the pure point spectrum \(\{\log p\}_{p\ \text{prime}}\), each eigenvalue simple, plus (since the primes are unbounded but not equidistributed near any finite accumulation point other than \(+\infty\)) no further spectrum in any bounded interval.

Proof. Unitarity and the group law are immediate from \(|p^{it}|=1\), \(p^{i(t+s)} = p^{it}p^{is}\). Strong continuity: for fixed \(w \in \ell^2\), \(\|\Lambda_t w - w\|^2 = \sum_p |p^{it}-1|^2|w_p|^2 \to 0\) as \(t\to0\) by dominated convergence (\(|p^{it}-1|\le2\), pointwise \(\to0\)). Each coordinate multiplier \(t\mapsto p^{it} = e^{it\log p}\) is generated by multiplication by \(\log p\); on a diagonal operator with distinct real diagonal entries \(\{\log p\}\) (primes are distinct positive reals with distinct logarithms), Stone's theorem gives the stated self-adjoint generator with pure point, simple spectrum exactly at those entries; boundedness away from any accumulation point in \([0,\infty)\) other than \(\infty\) is the elementary fact that consecutive prime gaps are unbounded but every bounded interval contains only finitely many primes. \(\blacksquare\)

Reading. This is the exact, rigorous sense in which "the primes carry a self-adjoint operator": the natural translation symmetry of the prime kernel is generated by multiplication by \(\log p\) on \(\ell^2(\text{primes})\) — a diagonal operator with spectrum \(\{\log p\}\), not the Riemann zeros. This is the geometric side of the explicit formula (Theorem 4 of research-findings.html): the trace of \(e^{itA}\) against a test measure produces the prime-sum side of the Guinand–Weil identity, while the zero-side sum \(\sum_\rho h(\gamma_\rho)\) is what a dual, zero-supported spectral object would need to reproduce. Constructing that dual object with a genuine, positive inner product — not merely as a distributional identity — is exactly the content of Hilbert–Pólya, and by Proposition 5.1 it cannot be found by any deformation internal to \(\mathcal H_P\): the generator here is pinned to \(\{\log p\}\) by unitarity of the translation flow alone.

7. Summary

The unnormalised prime kernel \(K(\lambda,\mu) = P(1-i(\lambda-\bar\mu))\) is an exact, positive-definite, holomorphic reproducing kernel on \(\mathbb C^+\times\mathbb C^+\), with a reproducing-kernel Hilbert space isometric to \(\ell^2(\text{primes})\) and a translation symmetry whose generator is diagonal with spectrum \(\{\log p\}\). It replaces the divergent / degenerate finite normalised prime kernels used in several Hilbert–Pólya proposals (see barry-keating-addendum-errata.html, Lemma L3 and Erratum E5) with the genuine object the primes support. It is the "geometric side" object of the Guinand–Weil explicit formula; its positivity on the boundary, in the precise sense needed to reach the zero side, is exactly Weil's criterion — proven equivalent to the Riemann Hypothesis, not a route around it (see hermite-biehler.html and research-findings.html §7–9).
