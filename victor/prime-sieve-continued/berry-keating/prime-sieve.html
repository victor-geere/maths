The Composite-Generator Prime Sieve
Rigorous Core

Research note — 3 July 2026

Provenance and scope. This document extracts the rigorous content of barry-keating-hamiltonian-sieve.html — namely its §§2–3: the composite-generator sieve, the normalised prime measure, and the equidistribution lemma — and completes the proofs. The source's remaining sections (its transfer operator, finite-stage operators, spectral identification, limiting characteristic function, de Branges construction, and adelic sketch, §§4–11) are not reproduced: their central claims are either ill-posed as stated (a multiplication operator credited with eigenvectors it does not have), degenerate (the \(1/\pi(M_n)\)-normalised transfer sum tends to zero uniformly — see the one-line estimate in §4 below), or asserted without proof. Everything that appears below is proven, with complete proofs on the page, and is verified numerically in §5. This document is self-contained.

1. The sieve

Definition 1.1 (composite generator).

For \(n \ge 1\) let \(I_n = [2^n,\, 2^{n+1}-1]\), let \(\mathcal P_n\) be the set of primes \(< 2^n\), and define \[ G_n = \{\, x \in I_n \;:\; x = p_1 p_2 \cdots p_k,\ p_i \in \mathcal P_n,\ k \ge 2 \,\}, \] where the product is over a multiset of primes (repetitions allowed). Write \(M_n = 2^{n+1}-1\) and \(k_n = \pi(M_n)\).

Lemma 1.2 (sieve correctness). proven

\(G_n\) is exactly the set of composite numbers in \(I_n\). Consequently \(I_n \setminus G_n\) is the set of primes in \(I_n\), and by induction all primes \(\le M_n\) are known after stage \(n\).

Proof. A product of \(k \ge 2\) primes is composite, so \(G_n \subseteq \{\text{composites in } I_n\}\). Conversely, let \(x \in I_n\) be composite and let \(x = q_1 q_2 \cdots q_k\) be its prime factorisation (\(k \ge 2\), multiplicities counted). For each \(i\), the complementary factor \(m = \prod_{j \ne i} q_j\) is an integer \(\ge 2\), so \[ q_i = \frac{x}{m} \le \frac{x}{2} < \frac{2^{n+1}}{2} = 2^n, \] i.e. \(q_i \in \mathcal P_n\). Hence \(x \in G_n\). For the induction: \(\mathcal P_1 = \emptyset\) declares \(I_1 = \{2,3\}\) prime; and if the primes \(< 2^n\) are known, stage \(n\) generates all composites in \(I_n\), exposing the primes in \(I_n\) as the gaps, which extends the known list to all primes \(< 2^{n+1}\). \(\blacksquare\)

Remark. Repetitions in the multiset are necessary: \(4 = 2\cdot2 \in I_2\) has no representation as a product of distinct primes below \(4\). The source states the sieve with a bare product \(p_1p_2\cdots p_k\); the multiset reading is the correct one and is what any implementation does anyway.

Proposition 1.3 (how many gaps: dyadic prime counts). proven

As \(n \to \infty\), \[ \#\{\text{primes in } I_n\} \;=\; \frac{2^n}{n\log 2}\,\big(1 + O(1/n)\big), \qquad k_n = \pi(M_n) \sim \frac{M_n}{\log M_n}. \]

Proof. The second statement is the prime number theorem. For the first, PNT with classical error gives \(\pi(x) = \mathrm{Li}(x) + O\big(x\,e^{-c\sqrt{\log x}}\big)\), and substituting \(t = 2^n u\), \[ \mathrm{Li}(2^{n+1}) - \mathrm{Li}(2^n) = \int_{2^n}^{2^{n+1}} \frac{dt}{\log t} = \frac{2^n}{n\log 2}\int_1^2 \frac{du}{1 + \frac{\log u}{n\log 2}} = \frac{2^n}{n\log 2}\big(1 + O(1/n)\big), \] while the PNT error terms are \(O(2^n e^{-c\sqrt{n}})\), negligible against \(2^n/n\). \(\blacksquare\)

Reading. The sieve's "gaps" are not sparse accidents: each dyadic block contributes \(\asymp 2^n/n\) new primes. The \(1 + O(1/n)\) correction is visible at computational sizes (§5, table B: the normalised ratio climbs \(0.94 \to 0.98\) between \(n = 12\) and \(n = 22\)).

2. The normalised prime measure

Definition 2.1.

On the circle \(\mathbb T = [0,1)\) define the empirical probability measure of the normalised primes and its Fourier coefficients at integer frequencies: \[ \mu_n = \frac1{k_n}\sum_{p \le M_n} \delta_{\{p/M_n\}}, \qquad \widehat\mu_n(k) = \int_{\mathbb T} e^{2\pi i k t}\, d\mu_n(t) = \frac1{k_n}\sum_{p \le M_n} e^{2\pi i k p/M_n}, \quad k \in \mathbb Z. \]

Only integer frequencies \(k\) are Fourier coefficients of a measure on \(\mathbb T\); the source occasionally writes \(\widehat\mu_n(\xi)\) with unrestricted \(\xi\), which should be read as the exponential-sum function of a real variable, not as a Fourier coefficient. Nothing below needs non-integer frequencies.

3. Equidistribution

Theorem 3.1 (vague convergence to Lebesgue, with rate). proven

For each fixed \(k \in \mathbb Z \setminus \{0\}\), \[ \widehat\mu_n(k) = O_k\!\left(\frac{1}{\log M_n}\right) \longrightarrow 0, \] and \(\widehat\mu_n(0) = 1\). Consequently \(\mu_n \to\) Lebesgue measure on \(\mathbb T\) in the weak-* topology.

Proof. Fix \(k \ne 0\) and write \(M = M_n\), \(e(x) = e^{2\pi i x}\). By Riemann–Stieltjes summation against the prime-counting function, \[ S := \sum_{p \le M} e\!\left(\frac{kp}{M}\right) = \int_{2^-}^{M} e\!\left(\frac{kt}{M}\right) d\pi(t), \qquad \pi(t) = \mathrm{Li}(t) + E(t), \] with \(E(t) \ll t\,e^{-c\sqrt{\log t}}\) (prime number theorem with the classical de la Vallée Poussin error). The error part contributes, after integrating by parts, \[ \int_{2}^{M} e\!\left(\frac{kt}{M}\right) dE(t) \;\ll\; |E(M)| + |E(2)| + \frac{2\pi|k|}{M}\int_2^M |E(t)|\,dt \;\ll\; (1+|k|)\,M e^{-c\sqrt{\log M}}, \] using \(\int_2^M |E(t)|\,dt \ll M^2 e^{-c\sqrt{\log M}}\) (the last dyadic block dominates). For the main part substitute \(t = Ms\): \[ \int_{2}^{M} \frac{e(kt/M)}{\log t}\,dt = M\int_{2/M}^{1} \frac{e(ks)}{\log(Ms)}\,ds . \] The range \(s \le M^{-1/2}\) contributes at most \(M \cdot M^{-1/2}/\log 2 = O(\sqrt M)\). On \(s \ge M^{-1/2}\), where \(|\log s| \le \tfrac12\log M\), expand \[ \frac{1}{\log(Ms)} = \frac{1}{\log M}\left(1 - \frac{\log s}{\log M} + O\!\left(\frac{\log^2 s}{\log^2 M}\right)\right) \] uniformly. The first term integrates to \(O(\sqrt M)\) because \(\int_0^1 e(ks)\,ds = 0\) exactly for integer \(k \ne 0\) (only the truncation at \(s = M^{-1/2}\) survives). The second term is \(\ll_k M/\log^2 M\), since \(\int_0^1 |\log s|\,ds = 1\). The third is \(\ll M/\log^3 M\). Altogether \(S \ll_k M/\log^2 M\), and dividing by \(k_n \sim M/\log M\) gives \(\widehat\mu_n(k) \ll_k 1/\log M\). Since trigonometric polynomials are dense in \(C(\mathbb T)\) and the \(\mu_n\) are probability measures, Weyl's criterion upgrades coefficientwise convergence \(\widehat\mu_n(k) \to \delta_{k,0}\) to weak-* convergence to Lebesgue measure. \(\blacksquare\)

Remark 3.2 (why the source's cited bound cannot be used).

The source derives Theorem 3.1 in one line from "Vinogradov's bound \(\sum_{p\le x} e(\alpha p) \ll_A x/\log^A x\) for \(0 < |\alpha| \le \tfrac12\)". No such bound holds for all \(\alpha\) in that range: at a fixed rational \(\alpha = a/q\) with \((a,q)=1\), splitting the primes into reduced residue classes mod \(q\) and applying the prime number theorem in arithmetic progressions gives \[ \sum_{p \le x} e\!\left(\frac{ap}{q}\right) = \sum_{\substack{r \bmod q \\ (r,q)=1}} e\!\left(\frac{ar}{q}\right) \pi(x;q,r) + O(q) = \frac{c_q(a)}{\varphi(q)}\,\frac{x}{\log x}\,(1+o(1)), \] where \(c_q(a) = \mu(q)\) is the Ramanujan sum. For \(q = 3\) this is \(-\tfrac12\,x/\log x\) — of full order \(x/\log x\), not \(o(x/\log^A x)\). Vinogradov-type savings require \(\alpha\) on a minor arc; the frequencies \(k/M_n\) appearing here are rationals (with large denominator \(M_n/\gcd(k,M_n)\), which is why the theorem is nevertheless true), and the PNT-with-partial-summation route above is the correct proof. The rate \(O_k(1/\log M_n)\) obtained is sharp up to constants at computational sizes (§5, table C).

4. What is omitted, and the one-line reason

The source proceeds to build a "transfer operator" \((\mathcal T_n a)(\gamma) = \frac1{k_n}\sum_{p\le M_n} a_p\, p^{-1/2+i\gamma}\) and, on top of it, finite-stage operators and a limiting spectral identification. None of that is reproduced here. Beyond the ill-posed operator definitions, the normalisation itself is degenerate: for any bounded coefficients \(\|a\|_\infty \le 1\) and every real \(\gamma\), \[ \big|(\mathcal T_n a)(\gamma)\big| \;\le\; \frac1{k_n}\sum_{p \le M_n} p^{-1/2} \;\sim\; \frac{2\sqrt{M_n}/\log M_n}{M_n/\log M_n} \;=\; \frac{2}{\sqrt{M_n}} \;\longrightarrow\; 0, \] uniformly on \(\mathbb R\) (the middle estimate is partial summation against Proposition 1.3's counting). Whatever limit theory one hopes to attach to \(\mathcal T_n\), its uniform limit on the real line is the zero operator; the sections of the source that assume otherwise are omitted for that reason.

6. Summary

The composite-generator sieve is correct (Lemma 1.2), quantitatively productive (Proposition 1.3), and the normalised prime measure it produces equidistributes on the circle with rate \(O_k(1/\log M_n)\) (Theorem 3.1, complete proof, sharp at computational sizes). This — no more and no less — is the rigorous content of the source document. Anything built on the \(1/k_n\)-normalised prime sums beyond measure-level statements collapses under the normalisation (§4); constructions that want an operator or a kernel from the primes must start from unnormalised, absolutely convergent objects instead.
