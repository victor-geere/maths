Abstract

The geometric reconstruction walks a fixed harmonic menu of steps \(\pi/2, \pi/3, \pi/4, \dots\), accepting or rejecting each once, and converges at the linear rate \(\pi/(n+1)\). Here we replace the fixed menu by the greedy harmonic choice: at every stage we advance by the largest admissible unit fraction of the half-turn, \(\pi/q\) with \(q = \lceil 1/r\rceil\), where \(r\) is the remaining fraction of the target. This is exactly the Fibonacci–Sylvester (Egyptian-fraction) expansion \(y = \sum_k 1/q_k\) applied to the angle. Two things are gained. First, convergence becomes doubly exponential: the error after \(k\) rotations is below \(\pi\,2^{-2^{k-1}}\), so six or seven square-root rotations exhaust double precision where the geometric version needs hundreds of steps for three digits. Second, for every rational \(y = a/b\) the expansion terminates in at most \(a\) steps, so the algorithm ceases to be a limit and becomes a closed form: a finite product of rotations \(\sin(\pi y) = \mathrm{Im}\prod_{k} e^{i\pi/q_k}\), exact, with each factor a sine of a unit fraction of \(\pi\) obtained from Pythagoras alone. We prove correctness, derive the closed form, and include live Plotly graphs showing the full 360° wave together with the number of rotations actually used.

4. Main Theorem and Proof

Fix \(x \in \mathbb{R}\) and set \(y = |x| \bmod 2 \in [0,2)\). Since \(\sin(\pi x) = \mathrm{sgn}(x)\sin(\pi|x|)\) and \(\sin(\pi\,\cdot\,)\) is \(2\)-periodic, it suffices to construct \(\sin(\pi y)\); and since \(\sin(\pi y) = -\sin(\pi(y-1))\) for \(y\in[1,2)\), it suffices to treat \(t \in [0,1)\) (the endpoints \(t=0\) and \(y=1\) give \(\sin = 0\) directly).

Lemma (Greedy Unit-Fraction Invariant). Let \(t \in (0,1)\). Define \(r_0 = t\) and, for \(k \ge 0\) while \(r_k > 0\), \[ q_{k+1} = \left\lceil \frac{1}{r_k} \right\rceil, \qquad r_{k+1} = r_k - \frac{1}{q_{k+1}}. \] Then for every \(k\) for which the process is still running:
  1. \(q_1 \ge 2\), and \(q_{k+1} \ge q_k(q_k-1) + 1 > q_k\) — the denominators grow at least like Sylvester's sequence \(2, 3, 7, 43, 1807, \dots\);
  2. \(0 \le r_k < \dfrac{1}{q_k(q_k-1)}\);
  3. if \(t = a/b\) is rational, the process terminates (\(r_K = 0\)) after \(K \le a\) steps, yielding the exact finite expansion \(t = \sum_{k=1}^{K} 1/q_k\);
  4. \(r_k < 2^{-2^{k-1}}\) for \(k \ge 2\); in particular \(\theta_k := \pi\sum_{j \le k} 1/q_j \to \pi t\) doubly exponentially.

Proof of Lemma. Since \(0 < r_0 = t < 1\) we have \(1/r_0 > 1\), so \(q_1 \ge 2\). The ceiling \(q = \lceil 1/r \rceil\) satisfies \(q \ge 1/r\) and \(q - 1 < 1/r\), i.e. \[ \frac{1}{q} \;\le\; r \;<\; \frac{1}{q-1}, \] so the step \(1/q\) is admissible (never overshoots) and \[ 0 \;\le\; r' = r - \frac{1}{q} \;<\; \frac{1}{q-1} - \frac{1}{q} = \frac{1}{q(q-1)}, \] which is (2). For (1): if \(r_k > 0\), then by (2), \(1/r_k > q_k(q_k-1)\), and since \(q_{k+1} = \lceil 1/r_k \rceil\) is an integer strictly greater than the integer \(q_k(q_k-1)\), we get \(q_{k+1} \ge q_k(q_k-1) + 1\); this exceeds \(q_k\) because \(q_k \ge 2\). (Equality throughout generates precisely Sylvester's sequence \(s_{k+1} = s_k^2 - s_k + 1\).)

For (3) (Fibonacci–Sylvester): write \(r_k = a_k / b_k\) in any representation with positive integers. Then \[ r_{k+1} = \frac{a_k q_{k+1} - b_k}{b_k q_{k+1}}, \qquad\text{with numerator}\quad 0 \le a_k q_{k+1} - b_k < a_k, \] where the right inequality is \(q_{k+1} < b_k/a_k + 1\), i.e. the ceiling property. The numerators are non-negative integers that strictly decrease, so at most \(a_0 = a\) steps occur before the remainder hits \(0\) exactly.

For (4): set \(a_k = q_k - 1 \ge 1\). By (1), \(a_{k+1} \ge q_k(q_k - 1) = (a_k+1)a_k \ge a_k^2\), and \(a_2 \ge q_1(q_1-1) \ge 2\), hence \(a_k \ge 2^{2^{k-2}}\) for \(k \ge 2\). Then by (2), \[ r_k < \frac{1}{q_k(q_k-1)} = \frac{1}{(a_k+1)a_k} < a_k^{-2} \le 2^{-2^{k-1}}. \qquad\blacksquare \]

Contrast with the geometric version: there the step menu is fixed in advance and the remainder obeys only \(r_n \le \pi/(n+1)\). Here the step is chosen — always the largest unit fraction that fits — and the remainder collapses doubly exponentially. It is the same greedy idea applied to the harmonic family \(\{1/q\}\) rather than to a predetermined schedule.

Theorem (Harmonic Reconstruction of Sine). With \(q_k, r_k, \theta_k\) as in the Lemma, define the rotation pair \((s_k, c_k)\) by \[ \begin{align} s_0 &= 0, \qquad c_0 = 1, \\[4pt] h_k &= \mathrm{dyadic\_sin}\!\left(\tfrac{1}{q_k}\right), \qquad c^h_k = \sqrt{1-h_k^2} \quad \Bigl(\text{valid since } q_k \ge 2 \Rightarrow 0 < \tfrac{\pi}{q_k} \le \tfrac{\pi}{2}\Bigr), \\[4pt] (s_{k}, c_{k}) &= \bigl(s_{k-1} c^h_k + c_{k-1} h_k,\ \ c_{k-1} c^h_k - s_{k-1} h_k\bigr). \end{align} \] Then \(s_k = \sin\theta_k\) and \(c_k = \cos\theta_k\) exactly, for every \(k\), and \[ |s_k - \sin(\pi t)| \;\le\; \pi r_k \;<\; \pi\, 2^{-2^{k-1}} \quad (k \ge 2). \] Consequently \(\sin(\pi t) = \lim_{k} s_k\), the limit being attained after finitely many steps whenever \(t\) is rational.

Proof.

\(s_k = \sin\theta_k\) and \(c_k = \cos\theta_k\) hold by induction exactly as in the geometric version: each update is the sine/cosine angle-addition formula for \(\theta_{k-1} + \pi/q_k = \theta_k\). The one square root taken, \(c^h_k = \sqrt{1-h_k^2}\), is applied only to the step angle \(\pi/q_k \in (0, \pi/2]\), whose cosine is genuinely non-negative — the cosine of the accumulated angle \(\theta_k\) is never reconstructed from \(s_k\) but carried forward exactly, so no sign is lost after \(\theta_k\) passes \(\pi/2\). Convergence follows from \(|\sin a - \sin b| \le |a - b|\) and the Lemma: \(|s_k - \sin(\pi t)| = |\sin\theta_k - \sin(\pi t)| \le \pi r_k\). When \(t\) is rational the Lemma gives \(r_K = 0\) after finitely many steps, so \(s_K = \sin(\pi t)\) exactly. \(\blacksquare\)

5. Closed Form

Because the greedy expansion terminates on rationals, the algorithm admits a closed form that the geometric version does not: the limit is a finite product of explicit rotations. Writing each rotation as a complex number of unit modulus, \(e^{i\pi/q} = \cos(\pi/q) + i \sin(\pi/q)\), the recursion for \((s_k, c_k)\) is exactly complex multiplication, so:

Closed form. Let \(y = |x| \bmod 2\), \(m = \lfloor y \rfloor \in \{0,1\}\), \(t = y - m \in [0,1)\), and let \(t = \sum_{k=1}^{K} 1/q_k\) be the greedy unit-fraction expansion of the Lemma (empty when \(t = 0\); \(K < \infty\) iff \(t\) is rational). Then \[ \boxed{\ \sin(\pi x) \;=\; \mathrm{sgn}(x)\,(-1)^m\; \mathrm{Im} \prod_{k=1}^{K} \exp\!\left(\frac{i\pi}{q_k}\right). \ } \] Expanding the product, the imaginary part is the odd-subset sum \[ \mathrm{Im} \prod_{k=1}^{K} \Bigl(c^h_k + i h_k\Bigr) = \sum_{\substack{S \subseteq \{1,\dots,K\} \\ |S| \text{ odd}}} (-1)^{\frac{|S|-1}{2}} \prod_{k \in S} h_k \prod_{k \notin S} c^h_k, \qquad h_k = \sin\frac{\pi}{q_k},\quad c^h_k = \sqrt{1-h_k^2}. \]

For rational \(x\) this is a finite, exact expression: the only ingredients are the integers \(q_k\) (produced by ceiling divisions), the sines of unit fractions \(\sin(\pi/q_k)\), and Pythagoras. In the implementation each \(\sin(\pi/q_k)\) is itself delivered by the dyadic bisection subroutine — nothing but halvings and square roots — so the approximation error lives entirely in those factors (\(\le \pi/2^{61}\) each with \(60\) bisections), not in the structure of the formula.

Worked examples

\(t = \tfrac34 = \tfrac12 + \tfrac14\) \((q_1,q_2) = (2,4)\): \[ \sin\tfrac{3\pi}{4} = \mathrm{Im}\left(e^{i\pi/2} e^{i\pi/4}\right) = \sin\tfrac{\pi}{2}\cos\tfrac{\pi}{4} + \cos\tfrac{\pi}{2}\sin\tfrac{\pi}{4} = \frac{\sqrt2}{2}. \] \(t = \tfrac56 = \tfrac12 + \tfrac13\) \((q_1,q_2) = (2,3)\): \[ \sin\tfrac{5\pi}{6} = \mathrm{Im}\left(e^{i\pi/2} e^{i\pi/3}\right) = \cos\tfrac{\pi}{3} = \tfrac12. \] \(t = \tfrac45 = \tfrac12 + \tfrac14 + \tfrac1{20}\) \((q_1,q_2,q_3) = (2,4,20)\): \[ \sin\tfrac{4\pi}{5} = \mathrm{Im}\left(e^{i\pi/2} e^{i\pi/4} e^{i\pi/20}\right) = \cos\!\left(\tfrac{\pi}{4} + \tfrac{\pi}{20}\right) = \cos\tfrac{3\pi}{10}. \] And the infinite showcase: the greedy expansion of \(1\) by proper unit fractions is Sylvester's series \(1 = \tfrac12 + \tfrac13 + \tfrac17 + \tfrac1{43} + \tfrac1{1807} + \cdots\), whence the identity \[ \mathrm{Im} \prod_{k=1}^{\infty} \exp\!\left(\frac{i\pi}{s_k}\right) = \sin\pi = 0, \qquad s_{k+1} = s_k^2 - s_k + 1,\ s_1 = 2. \]

History

This is the harmonic companion to A Purely Geometric Reconstruction of the Sine Function (sine-geometric.html): the same rotation engine — carry the pair \((\sin\theta, \cos\theta)\), advance by angle addition, extract every step sine from Pythagorean bisection — driven by the greedy Egyptian-fraction choice of step instead of a fixed step schedule. The original geometric construction was first published by Victor Geere in 2020: https://victorgeere.co.za/sine/index.html
