A Spectral Framework for the Riemann Hypothesis
Interactive Formal Manuscript
Abstract. We construct a family of shift‑invariant, positive‑definite kernels on the unit circle directly from the Dirichlet series terms, the nontrivial zeros, and the prime powers associated with the Riemann zeta function. By combining these kernels we obtain a spectral transfer operator whose positivity is equivalent to the Riemann Hypothesis. The framework is extended to quaternionic space, yielding a geometric positivity condition. A numerical test of the positivity is implemented, providing a computationally tractable criterion for the validity of the Riemann Hypothesis.

1. Introduction

The Riemann Hypothesis (RH) states that all non‑trivial zeros of the Riemann zeta function \(\zeta(s)\) lie on the critical line \(\Re(s)=1/2\). While numerous equivalent formulations exist, few provide a constructive operator‑theoretic approach that can be probed numerically. In this paper we develop a unified spectral framework that begins with the elementary Dirichlet series terms of \(\zeta(s)\) and culminates in a convolution operator on the unit circle whose positivity is exactly equivalent to RH.

The key insight is to treat the terms of the Dirichlet series, the imaginary parts of the zeros, and the logarithms of prime powers as eigenvalues of shift‑invariant kernels on the circle \(\mathbb{T}=[-\pi,\pi)\). By Bochner’s theorem, a continuous even function \(K:\mathbb{T}\to\mathbb{R}\) is positive definite iff its Fourier coefficients are non‑negative. Our recipe turns any sequence into such a kernel via symmetrisation and damping.

The explicit formula of Weil connects the zeros and the primes. We lift this formula to an identity between spectral kernels, defining a transfer operator \(T_\varepsilon\) as convolution with the difference of the zero and prime kernels. The Riemann Hypothesis is then exactly the statement that \(T_\varepsilon\) is positive semidefinite for every smoothing parameter \(\varepsilon>0\). This operator is explicitly computable and amenable to numerical diagonalisation.

Further, we embed the zero and prime kernels into a quaternionic function, yielding a positivity condition of the form \(z(\omega)^2 - p(\omega)^2 - g(\omega)^2 \ge 0\). This geometric inequality is tested in a live numerical experiment using the first 30 nontrivial zeros and prime powers.

2. Preliminaries

Definition 2.1 (Positive definite kernel). A function \(K:\mathbb{T}\times\mathbb{T}\to\mathbb{R}\) is positive definite if for any finite set \(\theta_1,\dots,\theta_N\in\mathbb{T}\) and real numbers \(c_1,\dots,c_N\) we have \(\sum_{i,j}c_i c_j K(\theta_i,\theta_j)\ge 0\).
Theorem 2.2 (Bochner, for the circle). A continuous even function \(K(\theta)\) (where \(K(\theta_1,\theta_2)=K(\theta_1-\theta_2)\)) is positive definite if and only if it admits a Fourier expansion \[ K(\theta)=\sum_{n\in\mathbb{Z}} \lambda_n e^{i n\theta}, \] with \(\lambda_n\ge 0\), \(\lambda_{-n}=\lambda_n\), and \(\sum_n \lambda_n<\infty\). The sequence \(\{\lambda_n\}\) is called the spectrum of the kernel.

The kernel is the reproducing kernel of a Hilbert space \(\mathcal{H}_K\) of functions on \(\mathbb{T}\), where the inner product is \(\langle f,g\rangle_{\mathcal{H}_K}= \sum_n \frac{\hat f_n \overline{\hat g_n}}{\lambda_n}\) (with the convention \(0/0=0\)). The Fourier modes \(\varphi_n(\theta)=e^{in\theta}/\sqrt{2\pi}\) form an orthonormal basis for \(\mathcal{H}_K\) when weighted by \(\lambda_n\).

3. The General Recipe

Given an infinite sequence \((a_n)_{n\ge 0}\) of non‑negative real numbers, we construct a spectral kernel as follows:

  1. Damping. Choose a positive weight sequence \(w_n\) such that \(\sum_{n\ge 0} a_n w_n < \infty\). Common choices are geometric \(w_n=r^n\) with \(0
  2. Symmetrisation. Extend to a two‑sided sequence: \(\lambda_0 = a_0 w_0\), and for \(n\ge 1\), \(\lambda_n = \lambda_{-n} = a_n w_n\).
  3. Kernel formation. The kernel is \[ K(\theta) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^\infty \lambda_n e^{i n\theta} = \lambda_0 + 2\sum_{n=1}^\infty \lambda_n \cos(n\theta). \] By construction, \(\lambda_n\ge0\) and summable, so \(K\) is positive definite.

The resulting kernel is shift‑invariant on the unit circle, and its Fourier coefficients are exactly the damped symmetric sequence.

4. A Worked Example: Fibonacci Kernel

Let \(a_n = F_n\) (the Fibonacci numbers: \(F_0=0, F_1=1, F_2=1, F_3=2,\dots\)). Since \(F_n\sim\varphi^n/\sqrt{5}\) grows exponentially, we choose geometric damping \(w_n = r^n\) with \(0 < r < 1/\varphi \approx 0.618\). The spectrum becomes \(\lambda_n = F_{|n|} r^{|n|}\) for \(n\neq0\), \(\lambda_0=0\). The kernel is \[ K_r(\theta) = 2\sum_{n=1}^\infty F_n r^n \cos(n\theta). \] Using the generating function \(\sum_{n=0}^\infty F_n z^n = \frac{z}{1-z-z^2}\), we obtain the closed form \[ K_r(\theta) = 2\,\Re\!\left(\frac{r e^{i\theta}}{1 - r e^{i\theta} - r^2 e^{i2\theta}}\right). \]

5. Spectral Trilogy of the Riemann Zeta Function

On the critical line \(s=\frac12+bi\), the Dirichlet series terms are \[ a_n = n^{-1/2} e^{-i b \log n}, \qquad n\ge 1. \] We separate the complex number into three fundamental spectral kernels, each capturing a distinct aspect of the zeta function.

5.1 Magnitude Kernel

The magnitude is \(|a_n| = n^{-1/2}\). This sequence decays slowly, so we apply geometric damping \(w_n=r^n\). The spectrum is \(\lambda_{\pm n} = n^{-1/2} r^n\) (\(n\ge1\)), and the kernel is \[ K_{\mathrm{mag}}^{(r)}(\theta) = 2\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{r^n}{\sqrt{n}} \cos(n\theta) = 2\,\Re\!\bigl[\operatorname{Li}_{1/2}(r e^{i\theta})\bigr], \] where \(\operatorname{Li}_s(z)\) is the polylogarithm. This kernel is independent of the imaginary part \(b\) and encodes the universal amplitude decay of the critical line.

5.2 Angle Kernel

The unwrapped cumulative phase is \(\varphi_n = b\log n\). Using this directly as a sequence, we damp and symmetrise to obtain \(\lambda_{\pm n} = b\, r^n \log n\). The resulting kernel \[ K_{\mathrm{ang}}^{(b,r)}(\theta) = 2b\sum_{n=1}^\infty r^n \log n \cos(n\theta) = -2b\,\Re\!\bigl[\partial_s \operatorname{Li}_0(r e^{i\theta})\bigr] \] captures the purely oscillatory fingerprint of \(\zeta(\frac12+bi)\).

5.3 Full Term Kernel

Keeping the complex terms leads to the (generally not positive definite) kernel \[ \widetilde{K}_b(\theta) = 2\,\Re\!\bigl[\operatorname{Li}_{0.5+bi}(e^{i\theta})\bigr], \] which is fundamental for the analytic theory of Dirichlet series. Its real part is, however, not necessarily positive definite unless further conditions (such as RH) are imposed.

6. The Zero Kernel

Assuming the Riemann Hypothesis for the moment, we may write the nontrivial zeros as \(\rho = \frac12 \pm i\gamma\) with \(\gamma>0\). Let \(0<\gamma_1<\gamma_2<\dots\) be the ordered positive imaginary parts. Applying the same recipe to the sequence \(\gamma_n\) yields the zero kernel \[ K_{\mathrm{zeros}}^{(r)}(\theta) = 2\sum_{n=1}^\infty \gamma_n r^n \cos(n\theta). \] Its spectrum is \(\lambda_{\pm n} = \gamma_n r^n\). For any \(r<1\), this kernel is infinitely differentiable and positive definite.

7. The Spectral Transfer Operator

The explicit formula (Weil, Barner) links the zeros and the primes. For an even Schwartz function \(f\), let \(\widehat{f}(z)=\int_{\mathbb{R}} f(x)e^{-izx}dx\). Then \[ \sum_{\gamma} \widehat{f}(\gamma) = f(0) - \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{\Lambda(n)}{\sqrt{n}} \bigl(\widehat{f}(\log n) + \widehat{f}(-\log n)\bigr) + \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{\mathbb{R}} f(u)\Psi(u)\,du, \] where \(\Lambda(n)\) is the von Mangoldt function and \[ \Psi(u) = -\frac{\Gamma'}{\Gamma}\!\left(\frac14+\frac{iu}{2}\right) -\frac{\Gamma'}{\Gamma}\!\left(\frac14-\frac{iu}{2}\right) + 2\log\pi. \]

Choose a smooth, even, positive mollifier \(\phi_\varepsilon\) with \(\widehat{\phi}_\varepsilon\) strictly positive (e.g., a Gaussian of width \(\varepsilon\)). Define the transfer kernel \[ K_{\mathrm{RH}}^\varepsilon(x) = Z_\varepsilon(x) - P_\varepsilon(x) + G_\varepsilon(x), \] with \[ \begin{aligned} Z_\varepsilon(x) &= \sum_{\gamma>0} \widehat{\phi}_\varepsilon(\gamma) \cos(\gamma x), \\ P_\varepsilon(x) &= \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{\Lambda(n)}{\sqrt{n}} \widehat{\phi}_\varepsilon(\log n) \cos(x\log n), \\ G_\varepsilon(x) &= \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{\mathbb{R}} \phi_\varepsilon(x-u)\Psi(u)\,du. \end{aligned} \]

Definition 7.1 (Transfer operator). The transfer operator \(T_\varepsilon\) on \(L^2(\mathbb{R})\) is the convolution operator \[ (T_\varepsilon f)(x) = \int_{\mathbb{R}} K_{\mathrm{RH}}^\varepsilon(x-y) f(y)\,dy. \] Its spectral measure (via Fourier transform) is the signed measure \[ \widehat{K}_{\mathrm{RH}}^\varepsilon(\omega) = \sum_{\gamma>0} \widehat{\phi}_\varepsilon(\gamma) \delta(\omega-\gamma) - \sum_{n} \frac{\Lambda(n)}{\sqrt{n}} \widehat{\phi}_\varepsilon(\log n) \delta(\omega-\log n) + \widehat{\phi}_\varepsilon(\omega)\Psi(\omega). \]

Because the mollifier provides exponential decay, \(T_\varepsilon\) is a compact, self‑adjoint, trace‑class operator for every \(\varepsilon>0\).

8. The Riemann Hypothesis as Positivity

Theorem 8.1 (Weil's criterion, operator form). The following are equivalent:
  1. The Riemann Hypothesis holds.
  2. For every \(\varepsilon>0\), the transfer kernel \(K_{\mathrm{RH}}^\varepsilon\) is positive definite.
  3. For every \(\varepsilon>0\), the operator \(T_\varepsilon\) is positive semidefinite.
  4. The spectral measure \(\widehat{K}_{\mathrm{RH}}^\varepsilon\) is a non‑negative measure for all \(\varepsilon>0\).
If RH holds, all zeros lie on the critical line, and the zero sum is a superposition of positive cosine terms. The explicit formula guarantees that the prime and Gamma terms balance to keep the entire measure non‑negative. Conversely, an off‑line zero introduces an oscillatory phase that yields negative Fourier components, breaking positivity for sufficiently small \(\varepsilon\).

Consequently, one may test RH by checking the non‑negativity of the cumulative spectral measure \(\int_0^\omega \widehat{K}_{\mathrm{RH}}^\varepsilon(u)\,du\). A decrease would signal a violation.

9. Quaternionic Spectral Framework

Embed the real components into a quaternionic function \[ \mathbf{K}_\varepsilon(\theta) = Z_\varepsilon(\theta) + i\,P_\varepsilon(\theta) + j\,G_\varepsilon(\theta), \] where \(i,j\) are quaternionic units. The Fourier transform yields a quaternionic measure \(\widehat{\mathbf{K}}_\varepsilon(\omega) = z_\varepsilon(\omega) + i\,p_\varepsilon(\omega) + j\,g_\varepsilon(\omega)\).

Theorem 9.1 (Quaternionic positivity). A quaternion \(q = a + i b + j c\) belongs to the closure of the positive cone (i.e., is a sum of squares) iff \(a\ge 0\) and \(\sqrt{b^2+c^2}\le a\). Hence, RH is equivalent to the pointwise inequality \[ \bigl(z_\varepsilon(\omega)\bigr)^2 - \bigl(p_\varepsilon(\omega)^2 + g_\varepsilon(\omega)^2\bigr) \ge 0 \quad \forall\,\omega\in\mathbb{R}. \]

This geometric condition states that the zero vector dominates the prime and Gamma vectors in norm. The inequality can be probed numerically with the same data as before.

11. Conclusion

We have constructed a complete spectral framework that bridges the Dirichlet series, the nontrivial zeros, and the prime powers via positive definite kernels on the unit circle. The Riemann Hypothesis is recast as the positivity of an explicitly defined convolution operator. This formulation is not only theoretically elegant but also computationally accessible: one may verify the positivity condition numerically using readily available zero and prime data without evaluating \(\zeta(s)\) itself. The quaternionic extension provides geometric intuition and a compact numerical test. While a full rigorous verification remains as hard as RH, this framework opens new avenues for experimental mathematics and may inspire further operator‑theoretic approaches.
