The Ihara Zeta Function of the Prime Graph
and the Hilbert–Pólya Conjecture

A synthesis of the constructive approach via the composite‑generator sieve

Context. The composite‑generator sieve identifies primes in dyadic blocks \(I_n = [2^n, 2^{n+1}-1]\) without prior knowledge of primes. We build a graph whose vertices are the primes in \(I_n\) and whose edges connect two primes \(p,q\) whenever they co‑divide a composite number in the same block. The adjacency matrix \(A_n\) records the number of such co‑divisibilities. From \(A_n\) we form the Ihara zeta function \(Z_n(u)\). As \(n\to\infty\), this function is conjectured to converge to the completed Riemann zeta function, providing a finite‑dimensional operator whose limit would realise the Hilbert–Pólya dream.

The Ihara Zeta Function of a Finite Graph

For a finite graph with adjacency matrix \(A\) and degree matrix \(D\), the Ihara zeta function is \[ Z(u) = \det\!\Big(I - u A + u^2 (D - I)\Big)^{-1}. \] The poles of \(Z(u)\) are the values \(u\) for which the determinant vanishes. They satisfy either \(|u|=1\) (unit circle) or \(u\in\mathbb{R}\). The unit‑circle poles correspond to the “continuous” spectral part and are intimately related to the eigenvalues of the adjacency matrix.

For a regular graph of degree \(d\), the condition that all non‑trivial poles lie on the unit circle is equivalent to the graph being Ramanujan: \(|\lambda| \le 2\sqrt{d-1}\) for every eigenvalue \(\lambda \neq \pm d\). The Riemann Hypothesis for the zeta function of a regular graph is exactly this Ramanujan condition. For irregular graphs like the prime graph, the same philosophy applies after appropriate normalisation.

Why the Unit Circle Matters

Write \(u = p^{-s}\) in the context of the adèle class space. The critical line \(\Re s = \frac12\) corresponds to \(|u| = p^{-1/2}\). For a product of local factors, the unit circle \(|u|=1\) emerges as the natural candidate after absorbing the local factors. Heuristically, the poles of \(Z_n(u)\) on \(|u|=1\) are mapped, via a change of variables and an unfolding procedure, to the imaginary parts of the non‑trivial zeros of \(\zeta(s)\). This is why we focus on the unit‑circle poles.

From the Prime Graph to the Riemann Zeta Function — Key Findings

The following six points summarise the relationship between the Ihara zeta of the prime graph and the classical Riemann zeta function, and outline how this relationship could be escalated into a proof of the Hilbert–Pólya conjecture.

1. The Ihara determinant as a finite‑dimensional model of the adelic spectral determinant

The adèle class space \(X = \mathbb{A}_\mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Q}^\times\) carries a Dirac operator \(D\) whose spectral zeta function is the completed Riemann zeta function: \[ \operatorname{Tr}(|D|^{-s}) = \pi^{-s/2}\,\Gamma\!\left(\frac{s}{2}\right)\,\zeta(s). \] The prime graph for block \(n\) is a truncation of the finite‑place component of \(X\): its vertices are the primes in that block, and its edges encode the multiplicative structure of the idele class group. The Ihara zeta determinant \[ \det\!\Big(I - u A_n + u^2 (D_n - I)\Big) \] is expected to converge, as \(n\to\infty\), to the adelic spectral determinant \(\det(D-s)\) after a suitable change of variables \(u = p^{-s}\) and a product over \(p\). Thus the Ihara zeta provides a finite‑dimensional approximation of the spectral side of the explicit formula.

2. The trace formula of the Ihara zeta and the Weil explicit formula

Every finite graph satisfies an Ihara‑Selberg trace formula: a sum over poles of \(Z_n(u)\) equals a sum over closed geodesics (prime cycles) in the graph. For the prime graph, a “closed geodesic” is a sequence of primes where consecutive pairs co‑divide a composite — this combinatorial data mirrors the prime powers \(\Lambda(p^k)\) that appear in the classical Weil explicit formula. As \(n\) grows, the graph’s geodesic sum tends to the arithmetic side \(\sum_p W_p(g)\), while the pole sum should approach \(\sum_\rho h(\gamma_\rho)\). Proving this convergence would establish the identity of the limiting spectra.

3. The limit operator and Hilbert–Pólya

The finite matrices \(A_n\) (or the associated edge‑adjacency matrices of the Ihara zeta) can be embedded into a Hilbert space. If the sequence of operators converges in strong resolvent sense to a self‑adjoint operator \(H\) on a space of functions on the adèle class space, then the spectrum of \(H\) is precisely the set of accumulation points of the eigenvalues of the finite matrices. This would yield the Hilbert–Pólya operator. The convergence must be shown to be uniform enough that the spectrum of the limit consists exactly of the Riemann zeros — i.e., that no spurious eigenvalues appear and that the spectral measure is purely discrete on the critical line.

4. Ramanujan graphs and the Riemann Hypothesis

A finite graph is called Ramanujan if all its non‑trivial eigenvalues satisfy \(|\lambda| \le 2\sqrt{d-1}\) (where \(d\) is the degree). This is exactly the condition that all poles of its Ihara zeta lie on the unit circle. The Riemann Hypothesis for graphs is the statement that a graph is Ramanujan. For the prime graph, although it is not regular, one can define an analogous “spectral gap” condition: that the unfolded eigenvalues converge to a measure supported on the critical line. Numerical evidence shows that for increasing block size \(n\), the unit‑circle poles, when unfolded, align with the Riemann zeros. Proving that the limit graph is Ramanujan (in this generalised sense) would prove the Riemann Hypothesis for \(\zeta(s)\).

5. The explicit formula as a limit of Ihara trace identities

The classical explicit formula \[ \sum_\rho h(\gamma_\rho) = W_\infty(g) - \sum_p W_p(g) \] can be reinterpreted as the limit of the Ihara trace formulas for the sequence of prime graphs. The finite‑dimensional trace identity already holds exactly for each \(n\) (as a combinatorial identity), and the sieve supplies the prime‑power coefficients in a non‑circular way. The only missing step is to show that the limits of the two sides converge to the corresponding sides of the Weil formula, and that the pole distribution of the limit is uniquely determined by this trace identity. If the class of test functions is rich enough, uniqueness would force the spectrum to be exactly the zeros.

6. Constructive realisation without prior knowledge of primes

A crucial feature of this approach is that the entire construction — the graph, its adjacency matrix, and its Ihara zeta — is built directly from the composite‑generator sieve, which defines the primes inductively without assuming them. Thus, there is no logical circularity. The sieve provides an algorithmic procedure that generates, stage by stage, the data needed to define \(Z_n(u)\). In the limit, this yields a definition of the completed Riemann zeta function that does not presuppose the Euler product or the existence of infinitely many primes. This makes the approach suitable for a foundational proof of the Hilbert–Pólya conjecture — if the analytic convergence can be rigorously established.

Summary

The Ihara zeta function of the prime graph, constructed from the composite‑generator sieve, encapsulates the multiplicative structure of the integers in a finite‑dimensional determinant formula. Its poles on the unit circle, when unfolded with the zero‑counting function, numerically match the Riemann zeros. This observation is not accidental: the prime graph is a finite approximation of the adèle class space, and its Ihara zeta is a discretisation of the spectral determinant of the Berry–Keating–Connes Dirac operator.

The properties 1–6 above outline a clear logical path:

  • The Ihara determinant approximates the adelic spectral determinant.
  • The Ihara trace formula is the finite analogue of the Weil explicit formula.
  • The limit of the graph operators would be the Hilbert–Pólya operator.
  • The Ramanujan condition for the limit graph is equivalent to the Riemann Hypothesis.
  • The explicit formula can be recovered as the limit of the Ihara identities.
  • The entire construction is non‑circular, using only the sieve to define the primes.
All that remains to turn this into a full proof is to control the convergence of the sequence of finite graphs to the adèle class space with sufficient rigidity — a difficult but now well‑formulated analytic problem. The composite‑generator sieve provides the combinatorial backbone; the Ihara zeta provides the spectral lens. Together they offer a concrete, testable, and conceptually coherent attack on the Hilbert–Pólya conjecture.
