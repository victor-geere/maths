1. The General Recipe — From Sequence to Spectral Kernel

Any infinite sequence \(a_n\) can be transformed into a shift‑invariant, positive definite kernel on the unit circle \(\mathbb{T}=[-\pi,\pi)\) by following three necessary conditions from Bochner's theorem:

ConditionMeaningAction
Non‑negativity\(\lambda_n \ge 0\) for all \(n\)Take absolute value, square, or shift the sequence
Symmetry\(\lambda_{-n} = \lambda_n\)Extend one‑sided to two‑sided: \(\lambda_{\pm n}=a_{|n|}\)
Summability\(\sum_{n}\lambda_n < \infty\)Apply damping \(w_n\) (geometric, Gaussian, etc.)
Recipe card:
① Input sequence \(a_n\) → ② Ensure \(a_n\ge 0\) → ③ Damp: \(a_n w_n\) with \(\sum a_n w_n<\infty\)
④ Symmetrise: \(\lambda_0=a_0w_0,\;\lambda_{\pm n}=a_{|n|}w_{|n|}\)
⑤ Kernel: \(K(\theta)=\sum_{n\in\mathbb{Z}}\lambda_n e^{in\theta}=\lambda_0+2\sum_{n=1}^\infty \lambda_n\cos(n\theta)\)

The resulting \(K(\theta)\) is a spectral kernel: its Fourier coefficients are the eigenvalues \(\lambda_n\), and it defines a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) on the circle.

3. The Riemann Zeta Trilogy — Terms, Angles, Magnitudes

For \(\zeta(\frac12+bi)\), the Dirichlet terms are \(a_n=n^{-1/2}\,e^{-ib\log n}\). We decompose into three separate spectral kernels, each capturing a different facet.

3.1 Magnitude Kernel (amplitude only)

Discard the phase: \(|a_n|=n^{-0.5}\). Damp with \(r^{|n|}\) (since \(\sum n^{-0.5}\) diverges).

\(\lambda_0=0,\quad \lambda_{\pm n}=|n|^{-0.5}\,r^{|n|},\quad K_r(\theta)=2\,\Re[\operatorname{Li}_{1/2}(re^{i\theta})]\)

3.2 Angle Kernel (cumulative phase only)

Use the unwrapped phase: \(\varphi_n=b\log n\). This isolates the oscillatory fingerprint.

\(\lambda_0=0,\quad \lambda_{\pm n}=b\,r^{|n|}\log|n|,\quad K_{b,r}(\theta)=-2b\,\Re[\partial_s\operatorname{Li}_0(re^{i\theta})]\)

3.3 Full Terms (complex, Hermitian)

The complete terms yield a conditionally positive definite kernel connected to the analytic theory of \(\zeta(s)\).

\(\widetilde{K}_b(\theta)=2\,\Re[\operatorname{Li}_{0.5+bi}(e^{i\theta})]\) — not positive definite but fundamental.

4. Zero Kernel — The Nontrivial Zeros as a Spectrum

Let \(\gamma_n\) be the positive imaginary parts of the nontrivial zeros (assuming RH). Apply the same recipe directly to the sequence \(\gamma_n\):

\(\lambda_0=0,\quad \lambda_{\pm n}=\gamma_n\,r^{|n|},\quad K_r^{\text{zeros}}(\theta)=2\sum_{n=1}^\infty \gamma_n\,r^n\cos(n\theta)\)

This embeds the zero ordinates as eigenvalues of a shift‑invariant kernel on the circle. The RKHS \(\mathcal{H}_r^{\text{zeros}}\) consists of functions whose Fourier coefficients satisfy \(\sum_{n\neq0}|\hat{f}_n|^2/(\gamma_{|n|}r^{|n|})<\infty\).

5. The Spectral Transfer Operator — Zeros ⇄ Primes

The explicit formula connects the zeros to the von Mangoldt prime‑power function. Define the Laplace‑regularised zero kernel and subtract the prime contribution:

\(K_{\text{RH}}^\varepsilon(\theta)= \underbrace{\sum_{\gamma>0}e^{-\varepsilon\gamma}\cos(\gamma\theta)}_{\text{Zero kernel}} \;-\; \underbrace{\sum_{n}\frac{\Lambda(n)}{\sqrt{n}}e^{-\varepsilon\log n}\cos(\theta\log n)}_{\text{Prime kernel}} \;+\;\text{smooth Gamma term}\)

The convolution operator \(T_\varepsilon\) with kernel \(K_{\text{RH}}^\varepsilon\) has a spectral measure that is the difference between the zero point measure and the prime point measure. This is the spectral transfer operator.

↑ Zeros (positive masses) ↓ Primes (subtracted masses)

6. The Riemann Hypothesis as a Positivity Condition

The perturbed kernel \(K_{\text{RH}}^\varepsilon\) is positive definite for all \(\varepsilon>0\) if and only if the Riemann Hypothesis holds. This is a rigorous equivalence:

RH ⇔ For every \(\varepsilon>0\), the kernel \(K_{\text{RH}}^\varepsilon(x)=\sum_\gamma e^{-\varepsilon\gamma}\cos(\gamma x)-\sum_n\frac{\Lambda(n)}{\sqrt{n}}e^{-\varepsilon\log n}\cos(x\log n)+\text{const}\) is a positive definite function on \(\mathbb{R}\).

If an off‑line zero existed, its complex weight would introduce an oscillatory phase that breaks positivity. The transfer operator \(T_\varepsilon\) thus provides a reliable, proven spectral criterion equivalent to RH — something the hypothetical Hilbert–Pólya operator has never achieved.
